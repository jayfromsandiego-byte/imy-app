01 · A life in full

Who she was, before what she did.

Begin with the person — the small, exact, beloved things — and let the timeline show how those values played out across eighty-two years.

“Find the smallest beautiful thing in the day, and tell someone about it. That's the whole point.”
— what she said, most mornings, for forty years
She loved most
Her garden in the hour before anyone woke. Letters in real ink. The same beach, every Sunday, for forty years. And second helpings — always second helpings.
Her morning
Black coffee, no sugar. She held it was the only civilized way to start a day.
Her kitchen
Vanilla and lemon zest. A lemon cake that no one has been able to repeat.
She always wore
Her mother's small pearl earrings, and a grey cardigan with a pen in the pocket.
Her values
Attention. Kindness. A thank-you note, handwritten, with actual sentences.
If you asked her
She would tell you the ordinary was never ordinary — that a ripe tomato, a child understanding multiplication, the light through a window, were each worth stopping for.

Three children. A wood-paneled station wagon. Cakes from scratch. Christmas mornings that began before dawn.

Lost Robert family
Fifty-two years of mornings together. The worst part, she said, was forgetting for half a second that he wasn't there to share something with.
2024

At home, surrounded by family. The Sunday paper still folded on the table. A vase of roses she had cut that morning.

03 · Letters on the wind

A word just for her — and stories for us all.

Two ways to speak to her memory. Send a private letter she alone receives, carried off by a dove. Or add a memory to the wall, where the rest of us can reminisce together — the funny, the sad, the unvarnished.

Private · for Eleanor only

Write a letter to Eleanor.

Whatever you didn't get to say. When you release it, a dove carries it slowly out of sight — about nine seconds. Slow, on purpose.
